      For the past years, many qualitative studies rely on lived experiences as a basis, especially those involving phenomenological reflection due to the ‘intelligibility’ both in its beginning and ending. This essentially comes from our awareness of it and its evaluative aspect, which as well features effective use in better understanding the human experience and other phenomena. As we focus on the fundamentality of lived experiences, personal insights and the public side of social are gained and interlinked. This tends to make it specially relevant to marginalized groups which families of EJK victims seemed to belong. The lived experiences of these people in accord with the issue regarding EJK might highly reflect and give rise to the meaning of the incidents themselves. This as well might then shed light to the social issue, and give voice to its victims.

      Extrajudicial killings (EJK) have been a persistent global issue since the early 1940s and result in the formulation of many local and international laws that aim to fully eradicate it (Pangilinan, 2012; Parreño, 2011; & Henckaerts, 2005). However, its cases continue to grow and it was reported to show an increase in its incidence around the world.

      In the Philippines, EJK remains the root of the continued international criticisms and started to become rampant due to the imposition of Martial Law. It had been used as a counterinsurgency strategy of the government and was allowed during the time of Arroyo and Aquino. It is also tolerated by Aquino’s successor, Rodrigo Duterte after a few months of being elected as a president.


      EJK seemed to be directed against the rampant illegal drug use in the country as for the present administration. In fact, as of January 9, 2017, a total of 4,146 cases of drug-related killings occurred outside police operations and 3, 271 of them were categorized as “deaths under investigation” (Bueza, 2016).

      This social issue seems to be viewed in two lenses of which it is perceived to be victim-centered and extended beyond the victims, having an immeasurable impact on their families. These aid a primary focus on the killed victims and their moral status since they were involved in illegal drugs. It debates whether the victim deserves condemnation or sympathy (Saunar, 2014; Casiño, 2016; Morallo, 2017).


      As for the cases of the families of victims subjected to drug-related EJK, the available studies consistently focused upon only three elements that constitute their experiences as a result of the loss of their family members. These are legal obstacles, economic difficulties and violation of emotional integrity (Clarke, 2016).

      The families of EJK victims experienced difficulties in investigation and prosecution of which the said issue is commonly seen in the apparent poor policing and the lack of cooperation and reluctance to testify on the part of the witnesses (Human Rights Watch, 2007; Human Rights Watch, 2011; Ateneo Human Rights Center, 2017;Amnesty International, 2017; Pangilinan, 2012). It also lacks formality, legislated and clear definition which makes it harder for prosecutors to solve and close cases due to an undefined scope of what kinds of killing constitute such.

      Since EJK victims are often identified as the breadwinners of their families, their death marked a greater economic suffering experienced by the families left behind (Center for Trade Union and Human Rights, 2011). It adversely affected their overall standard of living and resulted in diminished household income thereby having a significant impact on household budgeting and a family’s quality of life (Corden & Hirst, 2013).

      Given the fact that EJK often ends unresolved, the recovery of families from the death of a family member is made more difficult. It was found that even their emotional integrity was negatively affected having the loss of a family member who was killed without due process of law. To sum it up, EJK brought feelings of hopelessness, prolonged grief and fear to the families of victims.
